instructions:
  - title: Prep your fruits
    steps:
      - Pit the fruits and cut them into bite-size pieces.
      - Add the fruits to a big pot, cover them with sugar.
      - Turn on the heat until the sugar dissolves into a syrup.
      - Let the pot rest for the night.
  - title: Make the jam
    steps: 
      - Bring to a boil while stirring frequently, add lemon juice.
      - In another pot, start boiling some water with the empty jam containers and lids.
      - If you like your jam homogeneous and smooth, use a hand blender to blend everything once the fruits are soft and cooked through.
      - If you like your jam with bigger pieces, wait for the fruits to be soft enough, and take out 3/4 of the fruits. Set them aside in a big bowl. Keep on cooking the rest of the jam until the mixture clings to a spoon but falls off in clumps. Skim foam from top. Add the fruits back into the mixture and cook a few more minutes for it to heat up.
  - title: Fill and seal your jam containers
    steps: 
    - The pot with jam containers and lids should be boiling at this point.
    - With a long wooden spoon or fork, take out a container and lid.
    - Pour jam into it, leaving 1 cm from the top. Make sure to pour enough syrup for the fruits to be submerged. Quickly close the container and turn it around so that the container is now "sitting on its head".
    - Repeat the process until all the jam is in containers. Let them cool down at room temperature.
    - When cool, make sure to label the containers with the year. They can be kept for a few years.
faq:
  - question: How long can I keep this jam?
    answer: The jars can be kept in a cool room for up to a few years. Ideally, eat them within a year and make another batch the following year!
  - question: What is the white mold on the surface?
    answer: If opened and not eaten fast enough, some jars might develop a few spots of white mold. Many people scrape that mold off, and safely eat what is underneath. If the mold is brightly colored or if you have a weak immune system, it might be better to discard the jar.

The earliest known recipe for jam is from the 4th Century C.E. and can be found in _De Re Coquinaria_ (_The Art of Cooking_), a Roman cookbook by Marcus Gravius Apicius. This ancient recipe combines ripe apricots, white wine, _passum_ (a sweet wine sauce made with honey), peppermint, pepper, salt, cornstarch and a splash of vinegar.

Honey was soon replaced by sugar when sugarcane arrived in Persia between the the 3rd and 6th Century C.E. It took several more centuries for sugar to reach medieval Europe, where it remained an expensive luxury. As a result, jam became a status symbol for aristocrats and royalty.

It wasn't until the 18th century that sugar became much more affordable. Large quantities were imported from Caribbean plantations, where it was produced through slave labor.

Today, jam is a pantry staple around the world and remains one of the simplest and most popular ways to preserve fruit.
