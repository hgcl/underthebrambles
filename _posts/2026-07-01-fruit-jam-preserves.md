instructions:
  - title: Prep your fruits
    steps:
      - Pit the fruits and cut them into bite-size pieces.
      - Add the fruits to a big pot, cover them with sugar.
      - Turn on the heat until the sugar dissolves into a syrup.
      - Let the pot rest for the night.
  - title: Make the jam
    steps: 
      - Bring to a boil while stirring frequently, add lemon juice.
      - In another pot, start boiling some water with the empty jam containers and lids.
      - If you like your jam homogeneous and smooth, use a hand blender to blend everything once the fruits are soft and cooked through.
      - If you like your jam with bigger pieces, wait for the fruits to be soft enough, and take out 3/4 of the fruits. Set them aside in a big bowl. Keep on cooking the rest of the jam until the mixture clings to a spoon but falls off in clumps. Skim foam from top. Add the fruits back into the mixture and cook a few more minutes for it to heat up.
  - title: Fill and seal your jam containers
    steps: 
    - The pot with jam containers and lids should be boiling at this point.
    - With a long wooden spoon or fork, take out a container and lid.
    - Pour jam into it, leaving 1 cm from the top. Make sure to pour enough syrup for the fruits to be submerged. Quickly close the container and turn it around so that the container is now "sitting on its head".
    - Repeat the process until all the jam is in containers. Let them cool down at room temperature.
    - When cool, make sure to label the containers with the year. They can be kept for a few years.

However, Boermans says the earliest known recipe for jam is in the 'De Re Coquinaria' (The Art of Cooking) by Apicius, which was written in the 4th Century C.E. at the height of the Roman Empire. It features quince mashed in honey, which is quite unlike the jam we buy today.  

Honey was widely used to preserve food—and particularly fruit—globally, food writer Sarah B. Hood points out in her book, Jam, Jelly and Marmalade: A Global History. Honey was cheap, easy to find, and had some natural antibacterial properties. 

But sugar makes better jam. Sugarcane is native to Southeast Asia and it wasn’t until sugarcane was introduced to Persia at some point between the 3rd and 6th Century C.E. that jam as we know it today was made for the first time.
